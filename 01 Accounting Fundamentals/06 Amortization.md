# 06. Amortization

## Learning Objectives

After completing this topic, you should be able to:

- Define amortization.
- Differentiate amortization from depreciation.
- Identify assets that are amortized.
- Understand how amortization affects financial statements.

---

# Definition

Amortization is the systematic allocation of the cost of an intangible asset over its useful life.

In simple terms,

> Amortization spreads the cost of an intangible asset over the period it is expected to provide benefits.

Examples of intangible assets include:

- Patents
- Copyrights
- Software
- Licenses
- Customer Lists
- Trademarks (in certain cases)

---

# Why It Matters

Many businesses invest heavily in intangible assets.

Instead of recording the entire cost as an expense immediately, the cost is spread over the asset's useful life to better match expenses with the revenue generated.

---

# Key Concepts

### Amortization Applies to Intangible Assets

Unlike depreciation, which applies to tangible assets, amortization applies to intangible assets.

Examples:

- Software purchased by a company
- Patent rights
- Operating licenses

---

### Amortization is a Non-Cash Expense

The cash payment occurs when the intangible asset is purchased.

Recording amortization later does not involve any additional cash outflow.

---

### Not Every Intangible Asset is Amortized

Some intangible assets have an indefinite useful life and therefore are **not amortized**.

Instead, they are tested periodically for impairment.

Examples:

- Goodwill
- Some trademarks with indefinite lives

---

# Depreciation vs Amortization

| Depreciation | Amortization |
|--------------|--------------|
| Applies to tangible assets | Applies to intangible assets |
| Examples: Machinery, Buildings, Vehicles | Examples: Patents, Software, Licenses |
| Usually recorded using depreciation methods | Usually recorded using the straight-line method |

---

# Real-Life Example

A company purchases accounting software for **€60,000**.

Useful life = **5 years**.

Annual Amortization:

€60,000 ÷ 5 = **€12,000 per year**

Every year:

- €12,000 is recorded as an amortization expense.
- The carrying value of the software decreases.

---

# Financial Statement Impact

### Income Statement

Amortization appears as an expense.

Profit decreases.

---

### Balance Sheet

The carrying value of the intangible asset decreases over time.

---

### Cash Flow Statement

Since amortization is a non-cash expense, it is added back under Operating Activities.

---

# Common Interview Questions

### What is the difference between depreciation and amortization?

Depreciation applies to tangible assets, while amortization applies to intangible assets.

---

### Is software depreciated or amortized?

Purchased software is generally amortized because it is an intangible asset.

---

### Is goodwill amortized?

No.

Under IFRS and U.S. GAAP, goodwill is generally not amortized. Instead, it is tested periodically for impairment.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Amortization and depreciation are the same.

False.

They have the same accounting objective but apply to different types of assets.

---

❌ Every intangible asset is amortized.

False.

Assets with an indefinite useful life, such as goodwill, are not amortized.

---

❌ Amortization requires cash payments every year.

False.

The cash payment occurs only when the asset is acquired.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Amortization allocates the cost of an intangible asset over its useful life.
- It is a non-cash expense.
- It reduces accounting profit but not cash.
- Goodwill is generally not amortized.
- Amortization affects the Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement.
