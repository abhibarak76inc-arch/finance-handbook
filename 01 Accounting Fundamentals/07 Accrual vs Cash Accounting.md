# 07. Accrual vs Cash Accounting

## Learning Objectives

After completing this topic, you should be able to:

- Differentiate between accrual accounting and cash accounting.
- Understand when revenue and expenses are recognized under each method.
- Explain why most companies use accrual accounting.
- Identify how each method affects financial reporting.

---

# Definition

Accounting can record transactions using two different methods:

- **Accrual Accounting** – Records revenue and expenses when they are earned or incurred, regardless of when cash is received or paid.
- **Cash Accounting** – Records revenue and expenses only when cash is actually received or paid.

---

# Why It Matters

The accounting method determines when financial transactions are recorded.

Large companies generally use **accrual accounting** because it provides a more accurate picture of business performance.

Small businesses may use **cash accounting** because it is simpler.

---

# Key Concepts

### Accrual Accounting

Revenue is recognized when it is earned.

Expenses are recognized when they are incurred.

Cash movement does not determine when transactions are recorded.

---

### Cash Accounting

Revenue is recognized only when cash is received.

Expenses are recognized only when cash is paid.

---

# Example

Suppose a consulting company completes a project on **20 December** and sends an invoice for **€10,000**.

The customer pays on **15 January**.

### Under Accrual Accounting

Revenue is recognized in **December** because the service has been completed.

### Under Cash Accounting

Revenue is recognized in **January** because cash is received then.

---

Another example:

A company receives an electricity bill of **€2,000** in December but pays it in January.

### Under Accrual Accounting

Expense is recorded in **December**.

### Under Cash Accounting

Expense is recorded in **January**.

---

# Accrual vs Cash Accounting

| Accrual Accounting | Cash Accounting |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| Records transactions when earned or incurred | Records transactions when cash changes hands |
| Provides a more accurate picture of profitability | Easier to maintain |
| Used by most medium and large companies | Common among small businesses |
| Required under IFRS and GAAP for most companies | Limited use for external reporting |

---

# Financial Statement Impact

### Accrual Accounting

Creates additional Balance Sheet accounts such as:

- Accounts Receivable
- Accounts Payable
- Accrued Expenses
- Deferred Revenue

Provides a more complete Income Statement.

---

### Cash Accounting

No receivables or payables are recorded until cash moves.

Financial statements are simpler but may not accurately reflect business performance.

---

# Why Most Companies Use Accrual Accounting

Accrual accounting follows the **Matching Principle**, which states that expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenue they help generate.

This results in more meaningful financial statements.

---

# Common Interview Questions

### Which accounting method do public companies use?

Most public companies use **accrual accounting** because it is required under accounting standards such as IFRS and U.S. GAAP.

---

### Which method gives a better picture of company performance?

Accrual accounting.

---

### Can a profitable company have negative cash flow?

Yes.

Revenue may be recognized before customers actually pay.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Revenue always means cash has been received.

False.

Under accrual accounting, revenue is recognized when it is earned.

---

❌ Cash accounting is incorrect.

False.

It is a legitimate accounting method but is generally suitable only for smaller businesses.

---

❌ Accrual accounting guarantees positive cash flow.

False.

A company can report profits while experiencing cash shortages.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Accrual accounting records transactions when they are earned or incurred.
- Cash accounting records transactions only when cash moves.
- Most companies use accrual accounting.
- Accrual accounting provides a more accurate measure of business performance.
- Revenue, expenses, receivables, and payables are all linked to accrual accounting.
