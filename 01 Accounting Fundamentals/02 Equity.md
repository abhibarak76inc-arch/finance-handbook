# 02. Equity

---

# Why Should You Learn This?

After learning about assets and liabilities, a natural question arises:

> If a company sells everything it owns and pays off everything it owes, who owns what remains?

The answer is **Equity**.

**Equity represents the owners' financial interest in the business.**

It is one of the three fundamental components of the accounting equation.

---

# Definition

Equity is the residual interest in the assets of a business after deducting all liabilities.

In simple words,

> Equity is what truly belongs to the owners after all debts have been paid.

---

# Formula

**Equity = Assets − Liabilities**

or equivalently,

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

---

# Real-Life Example

Imagine you purchase a house worth **€500,000**.

You pay **€100,000** from your savings and borrow **€400,000** from the bank.

The house is your asset.

The mortgage is your liability.

Your ownership in the house is:

**€500,000 − €400,000 = €100,000**

That €100,000 is your equity.

Businesses work exactly the same way.

---

# Business Example

Suppose a company owns:

| Assets | Amount |
|--------|--------:|
| Cash | €40,000 |
| Equipment | €30,000 |
| Inventory | €30,000 |
| **Total Assets** | **€100,000** |

The company owes:

| Liabilities | Amount |
|------------|--------:|
| Bank Loan | €45,000 |
| Accounts Payable | €15,000 |
| **Total Liabilities** | **€60,000** |

Therefore,

**Equity = €100,000 − €60,000 = €40,000**

The owners collectively own €40,000 of the business.

---

# Components of Equity

Equity is made up of several parts.

## Owner's Capital

Money invested by the owner(s) into the business.

---

## Retained Earnings

Profits that the company keeps instead of distributing as dividends.

These profits accumulate over time.

---

## Share Capital

Money raised by issuing shares to investors.

Common in public and private companies.

---

## Reserves

Amounts set aside for specific purposes or retained within the business.

---

# How Does Equity Increase?

Equity increases when:

- Owners invest more money.
- The business earns profits.
- The value of certain assets increases (in some accounting situations).

---

# How Does Equity Decrease?

Equity decreases when:

- The business incurs losses.
- Dividends are paid to shareholders.
- Owners withdraw money from the business.
- Certain asset values decline.

---

# Why Equity Matters

Investors often look at equity because it reflects the owners' stake in the company.

Higher equity generally indicates that a larger portion of the company's assets is financed by the owners rather than by debt.

However, high equity alone does not necessarily mean the company is profitable or financially healthy.

---

# Equity vs Assets vs Liabilities

| Assets | Liabilities | Equity |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| What the company owns | What the company owes | What belongs to the owners |
| Future economic benefits | Future obligations | Residual ownership |
| Increase business resources | Create claims on assets | Represents owners' claim |

---

# Common Interview Questions

### Is equity an asset?

No.

Equity represents ownership in the assets after deducting liabilities.

---

### Can equity be negative?

Yes.

If liabilities exceed assets, equity becomes negative.

This is sometimes referred to as **negative net worth**.

---

### Does making a profit increase equity?

Yes.

Profits increase retained earnings, which are part of equity.

---

### Does taking a bank loan increase equity?

No.

A loan increases both assets (cash) and liabilities.

Equity remains unchanged.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### Equity is cash.

False.

Equity is not cash.

It is the owner's claim on the company's net assets.

---

### More equity always means a better company.

False.

A company may have high equity but poor profitability or weak cash flow.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Equity is the owner's residual interest in the business.
- Equity equals assets minus liabilities.
- Equity increases through profits and owner investments.
- Equity decreases through losses and withdrawals.
- Equity completes the accounting equation.

---

# Did You Know?

- Equity is also called **Net Assets**.
- Book Value of Equity is often different from Market Value.
- Public companies report shareholders' equity in every annual report.
