# 04. Expenses

## Learning Objectives

After completing this topic, you should be able to:

- Define expenses.
- Differentiate expenses from liabilities and assets.
- Understand how expenses affect profit.
- Identify common types of business expenses.

---

# Definition

Expenses are the costs incurred by a business to generate revenue or operate its day-to-day activities.

In simple terms,

> Expenses are the costs of running a business.

Examples include:

- Salaries and wages
- Rent
- Utilities
- Marketing and advertising
- Office supplies
- Insurance
- Depreciation
- Interest expense

---

# Why It Matters

Expenses directly reduce a company's profit.

A business may generate high revenue, but if expenses are too high, it may still incur a loss.

Managing expenses efficiently is essential for maintaining profitability and long-term financial health.

---

# Key Concepts

### Expense vs Asset

- **Asset:** Provides future economic benefit.
- **Expense:** Represents a cost consumed during the current accounting period.

Example:

Buying a machine is an **asset**.

The yearly depreciation of that machine is an **expense**.

---

### Expense vs Liability

An expense is the **cost incurred**.

A liability is the **amount still owed**.

Example:

Employee salaries for June = Expense.

If those salaries have not yet been paid, they also become a liability called **Salaries Payable**.

---

### Expense vs Cash Payment

An expense does not always involve an immediate cash payment.

Example:

A company receives an electricity bill in December but pays it in January.

The expense belongs to December, while the cash payment occurs in January.

---

# Types of Expenses

### Operating Expenses (Operating Costs)

Expenses related to the company's core business operations.

Examples:

- Salaries
- Rent
- Utilities
- Marketing
- Office expenses

---

### Non-Operating Expenses

Expenses not directly related to the company's primary business.

Examples:

- Interest expense
- Loss on sale of assets
- Foreign exchange losses

---

# Real-Life Example

A bakery earns €100,000 from selling bread.

During the month, it incurs:

- Flour and ingredients = €25,000
- Employee salaries = €30,000
- Rent = €8,000
- Electricity = €2,000

Total Expenses = €65,000

Profit = €35,000 (before taxes and other adjustments).

---

# Financial Statement Impact

Expenses appear on the **Income Statement**.

As expenses increase, profit decreases.

If an expense has been incurred but not yet paid, a corresponding liability (such as Accounts Payable or Salaries Payable) may appear on the **Balance Sheet**.

---

# Common Interview Questions

### Do expenses always involve cash?

No.

Some expenses, such as depreciation, do not involve an immediate cash outflow.

---

### Can expenses exist before payment is made?

Yes.

Under accrual accounting, expenses are recognized when they are incurred, not necessarily when cash is paid.

---

### Why are expenses important?

Because they directly affect a company's profitability and operational efficiency.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Expenses are the same as liabilities.

False.

Expenses represent costs incurred, while liabilities represent obligations to pay.

---

❌ Higher expenses always mean poor management.

False.

Some expenses, such as research and development or marketing, may support future growth.

---

❌ Every cash payment is an expense.

False.

Buying equipment with cash is purchasing an asset, not recording an expense.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Expenses are the costs incurred to operate a business and generate revenue.
- Expenses reduce profit.
- Expenses appear on the Income Statement.
- Expenses are different from assets, liabilities, and cash payments.
- Efficient expense management is critical for profitability.
