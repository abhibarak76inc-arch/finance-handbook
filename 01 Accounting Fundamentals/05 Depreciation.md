# 05. Depreciation

## Learning Objectives

After completing this topic, you should be able to:

- Define depreciation.
- Understand why depreciation is recorded.
- Identify which assets are depreciated.
- Explain how depreciation affects financial statements.

---

# Definition

Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life.

In simple terms,

> Depreciation spreads the cost of an asset over the years it is expected to be used.

Examples of depreciable assets include:

- Machinery
- Vehicles
- Buildings
- Computers
- Furniture

---

# Why It Matters

Businesses use assets for many years rather than consuming them all at once.

Instead of recording the entire cost as an expense in the year of purchase, depreciation spreads that cost over the asset's useful life.

This provides a more accurate picture of profitability.

---

# Key Concepts

### Depreciation is NOT a Valuation Method

Depreciation is an accounting method used to allocate the cost of an asset.

It does **not** necessarily represent the asset's current market value.

---

### Depreciation is a Non-Cash Expense

Cash leaves the business only when the asset is purchased.

Depreciation records the cost allocation each year without any additional cash outflow.

---

### Which Assets are Depreciated?

Assets with a limited useful life.

Examples:

- Machinery
- Vehicles
- Office Equipment
- Furniture

---

### Which Assets are NOT Depreciated?

Assets with an unlimited useful life.

Examples:

- Land
- Goodwill (instead tested for impairment)
- Certain investments

---

# Common Depreciation Methods

### Straight-Line Method

The asset loses an equal amount of value each year.

Example:

Machine Cost = €100,000

Useful Life = 10 years

Annual Depreciation = €10,000

---

### Declining Balance Method

Higher depreciation is recorded during the early years of an asset's life and lower depreciation in later years.

This method is commonly used for assets that lose value more quickly in their initial years.

---

# Real-Life Example

A company purchases machinery for €120,000.

Useful life = 10 years.

Using the straight-line method:

Annual Depreciation = €12,000.

Each year:

- Machinery remains on the Balance Sheet.
- Accumulated Depreciation increases.
- €12,000 is recorded as a depreciation expense on the Income Statement.

---

# Financial Statement Impact

### Income Statement

Depreciation is recorded as an expense.

Profit decreases.

---

### Balance Sheet

The asset remains recorded.

Its carrying value decreases over time through accumulated depreciation.

---

### Cash Flow Statement

Depreciation is added back in the Operating Activities section because it is a non-cash expense.

---

# Common Interview Questions

### Why is depreciation considered a non-cash expense?

Because the cash payment occurred when the asset was purchased.

No cash leaves the company when depreciation is recorded.

---

### Why isn't land depreciated?

Land generally has an unlimited useful life and does not wear out through normal business use.

---

### Does depreciation reduce taxes?

Yes.

Since depreciation is an expense, it reduces taxable income.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Depreciation means an asset loses market value.

False.

Depreciation is an accounting allocation, not a measure of market value.

---

❌ Every fixed asset is depreciated.

False.

Land is generally not depreciated.

---

❌ Depreciation involves a cash payment every year.

False.

The cash payment occurs only when the asset is purchased.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Depreciation allocates the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life.
- It is a non-cash expense.
- Depreciation reduces accounting profit but not cash.
- Land is generally not depreciated.
- Depreciation affects the Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement.
