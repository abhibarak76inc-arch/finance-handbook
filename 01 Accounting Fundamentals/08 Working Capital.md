# 08. Working Capital

## Learning Objectives

After completing this topic, you should be able to:

- Define working capital.
- Calculate working capital.
- Understand why working capital is important.
- Identify ways businesses manage working capital.

---

# Definition

Working Capital measures a company's ability to meet its short-term financial obligations using its short-term assets.

In simple terms,

> Working Capital represents the money available to run the day-to-day operations of a business.

---

# Formula

**Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities**

---

# Why It Matters

Working capital indicates a company's short-term financial health and liquidity.

A business with sufficient working capital can:

- Pay suppliers on time.
- Pay employee salaries.
- Purchase inventory.
- Continue daily operations without financial stress.

---

# Key Concepts

### Positive Working Capital

Occurs when Current Assets exceed Current Liabilities.

This generally indicates that the company can comfortably meet its short-term obligations.

---

### Negative Working Capital

Occurs when Current Liabilities exceed Current Assets.

This may indicate liquidity problems, although some businesses (such as supermarkets) can successfully operate with negative working capital due to rapid inventory turnover and immediate cash collection.

---

### Components of Working Capital

**Current Assets**

- Cash
- Accounts Receivable
- Inventory
- Short-term Investments
- Prepaid Expenses

---

**Current Liabilities**

- Accounts Payable
- Salaries Payable
- Taxes Payable
- Short-term Loans
- Accrued Expenses

---

# Example

A company has:

Current Assets

- Cash = €60,000
- Inventory = €50,000
- Accounts Receivable = €40,000

Total Current Assets = €150,000

Current Liabilities

- Accounts Payable = €70,000
- Short-term Loan = €20,000

Total Current Liabilities = €90,000

Working Capital

€150,000 − €90,000 = **€60,000**

The company has positive working capital of €60,000.

---

# Financial Statement Impact

Working capital is calculated using items from the **Balance Sheet**.

Changes in working capital also affect the **Cash Flow Statement**, particularly cash generated from operating activities.

---

# Why Investors and Management Care

### Investors

Investors use working capital to assess a company's short-term financial stability.

Consistently poor working capital may indicate liquidity risks.

---

### Management

Management monitors working capital to ensure sufficient cash is available for daily operations while avoiding excess idle resources.

Efficient working capital management improves cash flow and operational efficiency.

---

# How Companies Improve Working Capital

Businesses can improve working capital by:

- Collecting customer payments faster.
- Managing inventory efficiently.
- Negotiating longer payment terms with suppliers.
- Reducing unnecessary short-term expenses.
- Improving cash management.

---

# Common Interview Questions

### What is working capital?

Working capital is the difference between current assets and current liabilities.

---

### Why is working capital important?

It measures a company's ability to meet short-term obligations and finance daily operations.

---

### Is negative working capital always bad?

No.

Some businesses, particularly retailers and supermarkets, can operate successfully with negative working capital due to fast inventory turnover and immediate cash collection.

---

### Which financial statement is used to calculate working capital?

The Balance Sheet.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Working capital is the same as cash.

False.

Cash is only one component of working capital.

---

❌ Higher working capital is always better.

False.

Excessively high working capital may indicate inefficient use of resources, such as excess inventory or slow collection of receivables.

---

❌ Negative working capital always means a company is in trouble.

False.

It depends on the company's business model and industry.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities.
- It measures short-term financial health.
- Positive working capital generally indicates good liquidity.
- Efficient working capital management improves cash flow.
- Working capital is a key metric in corporate finance, FP&A, treasury, and manufacturing finance.
